Project Overview

A new sub-brand needed
an identity as bold as
its audience

Basicare — a globally recognised beauty label — was launching Rose by Basicare, a new cosmetics sub-brand targeting tweens, teenagers, and young adults aged 18–30, heavily shaped by Korean beauty culture, K-pop aesthetics, and social-first shopping habits.

The brand had products. It had a name. What it needed was a full digital presence — a visual identity that could stand next to global K-beauty brands, a shopping experience native to how its audience actually browses, and a brand guide and component set the team could hand off to marketers, creators, and developers.

I was brought in at Meridian Creative Solutions as the UI/UX and Brand Designer, responsible for the entire design lifecycle: research, moodboard, information architecture, wireframing, high-fidelity UI, AR feature planning, developer collaboration, and the Digital Brand & Media Guide.

Filter System — A UX Innovation

Standard eCommerce filters (size, colour, price) don't solve the beauty shopper's real problem. A woman looking for lashes needs to filter by eye shape, lash type, style, and length. I designed a beauty-specific filter taxonomy — this was a BA-driven insight translated directly into UX.

Filter Design Rationale

The filter system required me to act as a BA first — I had to understand the product catalogue deeply before I could design filters that were genuinely useful. I mapped all 30+ SKUs by type, then defined the filter taxonomy: Product Category → Lash Type → Eye Shape → Style → Length → Price Range → Pack Type → Lash Color → Availability. Each filter was validated against real browsing scenarios before implementation.

Design Decisions

Every decision had a
reason

Good design is defensible design. Here is why I made the choices that defined this product — and the business logic behind each one.

Mobile-first, always
The target audience — 18–30 year olds in Southeast Asia — primarily discovers and shops for beauty products through Instagram and TikTok. I designed every screen for 375px mobile first and scaled to desktop, ensuring the core experience was never a downsized afterthought.
Touch-optimised interactions, vertical scroll layouts, simplified navigation, bottom-sheet modals for filters and cart
Large product imagery = purchase confidence
Beauty shoppers can't touch or try products online. Visual trust is the only trust available. I used large, image-dominant product cards and full-bleed PDPs to show texture, detail, and quality — so users could make a confident purchase decision before adding to cart.
Larger imagery directly correlates to higher perceived quality and reduced purchase hesitation in beauty eCommerce
AR Try-On — reducing the biggest barrier
The biggest hesitation in lash purchasing is "will this look right on me?" The AR try-on feature (accessible via "Try It" on every eligible lash product card) was planned to answer that question before checkout. I designed the UX to keep it one tap away without disrupting the browsing flow.
AR Try-On addresses the #1 reason beauty shoppers abandon — uncertainty about how products will look on them
Soft luxury colour system
The brand needed to feel premium without being cold or unattainable to a younger audience. I chose rose pinks and petal tones over clinical whites or bold saturated colours. The palette signals warmth, femininity, and desirability — qualities the target demographic actively seeks in the brands they follow.
Warm tones create emotional connection; premium spacing signals quality without price-point intimidation
Minimal layout for an expressive brand
Despite playful, expressive branding, I kept page layouts restrained and clean. Whitespace became the luxury signal. Over-decorating would have created visual noise that competed with the products — the opposite of what a beauty brand needs. Products must be the hero.
Clean layouts keep products visible and make the site feel calm rather than crowded
Beauty-specific filter taxonomy
Generic eCommerce filters (size, colour, price) don't work for lash shopping. I designed a contextual taxonomy specific to the product — filtering by eye shape, lash type, style, length, and availability. This required deep product knowledge and stakeholder collaboration before a single filter UI was drawn.
Right filters reduce browsing friction, increase product discovery, and help users find the exact product for their specific look

Challenges & How I Solved Them

The hard parts of
building Rose

01

Balancing aesthetic richness with usability

Soft colour palettes risk poor contrast. Decorative elements compete with CTAs. The brand demanded visual beauty and functional clarity simultaneously — a tension that ran through every screen.

How I solved it

Used whitespace strategically as a luxury signal rather than decoration. Maintained strict typographic hierarchy so CTAs always read clearly against soft backgrounds. Tested contrast ratios at every stage and used the near-black (#222) to anchor key actions.

02

Premium branding for a young audience — without feeling childish

The audience is young (Gen Z / early Millennials) but the brand needs to feel trustworthy and premium enough to justify purchasing. Too playful risks feeling cheap; too serious loses the audience entirely.

How I solved it

Applied "soft luxury" styling — generous spacing, editorial photography direction, and modern typography that reads as elegant, not toy-like. The brand voice is youthful ("Confidence Starts With The Right Tools"); the visual structure is premium.

03

30+ product SKUs across a 375px mobile screen

A large, diverse product catalogue needs to be browsable without overwhelming users on small screens. Product-heavy layouts easily become cluttered and hard to scan.

How I solved it

Designed a 2-column product grid on mobile with consistent card sizing. Added visual category thumbnails (Lashes / Nails / Eyes / Face) as quick-filters at the top of the listing page. Surfaced the filter button prominently so users can narrow the catalogue without scrolling through everything.

04

Consistency across website, brand guide, and social media direction

The project scope included multiple deliverables — website, Digital Brand Guide, social direction, product catalogue design. Keeping all touchpoints visually aligned without a pre-existing system was a significant challenge.

How I solved it

Built a reusable design system in Figma — standardised spacing tokens, button styles, icon pack, component library — before designing any specific page. The Brand & Media Guide then codified these rules for creators and marketers working outside Figma.

Key Learnings

What building Rose
taught me

01
How the site feels matters as much as how it works
How the interface makes a user feel is as important as how usable it is. Trust and desirability are built through visual consistency, imagery quality, and aesthetic warmth — not just clear navigation flows.
02
Brand systems are a UX investment, not overhead
Investing time early in design tokens, component libraries, and spacing systems paid off significantly in the second half of the project — decisions were faster, consistency was automatic, and developer handoff was smoother.
03
BA thinking makes design rationale unbreakable
Grounding design decisions in business requirements and audience insights made them far easier to defend in client presentations. "Because it looks good" loses every stakeholder meeting. "Because research shows this increases purchase confidence" wins them.
04
Mobile-first is a prioritisation discipline
Designing mobile-first forced ruthless prioritisation — what content truly matters, which interactions are essential. The desktop experience was better because the mobile constraints had already resolved the hardest UX decisions.
05
Presenting design in the client's language builds trust
Framing moodboards and UI decisions in terms of brand goals, audience behaviour, and business outcomes — rather than aesthetic preference — won the trust of the CEO and Creative Director. Communication is a design skill.
06
New features still need to feel obvious
Planning the AR try-on showed me that advanced technology must be invisible in the user experience. If users have to think about how to use a feature, the feature has failed — no matter how technically impressive it is behind the scenes.
