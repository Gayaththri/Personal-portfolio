Project Overview

A real-world mobility
workflow with a trust problem

PickMe is Sri Lanka's leading ride-hailing platform, used by millions of daily commuters across Colombo and beyond. The service connects riders with tuk-tuks, cars, bikes, and delivery services — embedded into the daily lives of office workers, students, and late-night commuters.

But there's a problem that no amount of map smoothness or faster matching solves: the anxiety gap — the 2–6 minute window between driver acceptance and arrival, where users feel uncertain, impatient, and disconnected from what's actually happening.

This case study focuses on that specific experience. Not a full app redesign. A targeted, workflow-grounded UX intervention to rebuild trust where it's most fragile — during the ride tracking experience itself.

Problem Discovery

What users actually
feel during a ride wait

I analysed publicly available app reviews, observed real-world usage patterns, and applied UX heuristics to diagnose the specific breakdowns in the ride tracking experience.

📍
Opaque driver status
Users cannot tell whether a driver has accepted and is moving, has accepted but is stationary, is stuck in traffic, or has simply not started the route. The current UI shows a dot on a map — nothing more.
"I never know if my driver is actually coming. The dot just sits there."
⏱️
ETA feels unreliable
The estimated time of arrival updates inconsistently — sometimes jumping from 3 minutes to 8 minutes without explanation. This single UX failure destroys trust more than any other element in the experience.
"ETA said 2 min. It's been 9. No explanation."
🔔
Notification overload or silence
Users either receive too many generic push notifications ("Your driver is on the way") or go completely silent after booking — creating anxiety in both directions. There is no contextual, state-based communication system.
"I got 4 notifications saying the same thing. Nothing useful."
🔇
Breakdown in rider-driver communication
When delays happen, riders have no way to understand why, and calling the driver is the only option — which creates friction, especially for users in public or those who prefer not to call. There's no lightweight communication layer for status updates.
"Had to call to find out he was stuck. Just tell me that."

🔴 Critical Gap: Steps 3–5
Between driver acceptance and arrival, the user receives no meaningful status updates. The ETA recalculates silently — users see the number change but never understand why. Driver movement is shown on a map but without contextual status labels. This 2–6 minute window is where most cancellations happen and where trust is permanently damaged.
🟡 Secondary Gap: Notification System
The notification pipeline fires at booking confirmation and arrival — two bookend events — but is silent during the most anxiety-producing middle period. Users are either notified too much (spam) or too little (silence). There is no contextual, state-triggered communication system.

The Solution

Not a redesign.
A trust system.

The intervention targets the 4-minute anxiety gap with four specific design changes — each grounded in a workflow requirement identified through the BA analysis. The goal is not to make the app look different. It is to make users feel informed, safe, and in control during the most emotionally vulnerable moment of the ride experience.

01 — Contextual Driver Status System

New Feature

Instead of a silent dot on a map, users now see a live driver status label — Moving toward you, Stuck in traffic, Parked nearby, Looking for parking. These labels are derived from real movement data already available to the system, surfaced for the first time in the user interface.

BA Requirement → UX Feature
The driver's movement state (moving / stationary / parked) is already computed by the system for routing purposes. The BA insight was that this data existed but was never surfaced to the user. The UX solution required no new backend infrastructure — only a UI layer to communicate existing system state. This is the difference between a redesign and a requirements-informed design intervention.

02 — ETA with Confidence Indicator

New Feature

When ETA changes, most users interpret it as the system being unreliable. The redesign adds a confidence band and a contextual explanation — "ETA updated due to traffic on Galle Road" — turning a trust-breaking moment into a trust-building one.

ETA with confidence bandAfter
ETA change with contextAfter
Key Insight
The ETA number changing is not the problem. Users accept that ETAs change. The problem is the absence of explanation. "8 min" with no context breaks trust. "8 min — traffic on Galle Road" builds it. The same data, communicated with context, transforms a frustrating moment into a transparent one. This is fundamentally a content design problem as much as a UI problem.

03 — Ride Progress Timeline

New Feature

A persistent 5-step visual progress timeline — Booked → Matched → En Route → Arriving → Here — gives users a mental model of the ride lifecycle. This is the same pattern used by Amazon, Uber Eats, and FedEx to reduce "where is it?" anxiety across different domains.

Design Decisions

Every decision
had a requirement behind it

🔍
Surface existing data, don't create new data
Every piece of information shown in the redesign — driver movement state, traffic conditions, distance — already exists in the system. The BA insight was that the problem was not a data problem but a communication problem. No new backend work required for the core features.
Maximum impact with minimum technical debt — a key requirement in any real product decision
📊
ETA range over false precision
Showing "4 min" implies certainty. Showing "3–6 min" is honest. The confidence band communicates that ETAs are estimates affected by real-world variables — setting accurate expectations prevents the perceived unreliability that comes from a single number changing.
Honest uncertainty is more trustworthy than false precision — a fundamental content design principle
🧭
Mental models reduce anxiety
The 5-step progress timeline (Booked → Matched → En Route → Arriving → Here) gives users a mental framework for the ride lifecycle. When users know what comes next, the current state feels less uncertain. This pattern is proven across delivery, travel, and logistics UX.
Predictable progression reduces anxiety more than speed improvements — users tolerate delays better when they understand the system
🔔
Fewer, better notifications over more, generic ones
Notification fatigue kills trust as much as silence does. Each notification in the redesign fires once per state transition and says something new. The goal is that every notification a user receives makes them feel more informed, not more annoyed.
Quality over quantity — a notification that says nothing new trains users to ignore all notifications
🛡️
Safety as a first-class feature, not a footnote
For late-night users like Priya, the tracking screen is a safety interface. Driver name, plate number, movement status, and location are not nice-to-haves — they are safety requirements. The redesign surfaces all identity and movement data prominently, not buried in a submenu.
Safety-critical information should be on the primary screen, always visible, never require a tap to access
🎯
Targeted intervention over full redesign
The entire case study solves one screen, one workflow, one emotional moment. This is intentional — product teams at top companies value designers who can identify and solve a specific, high-impact problem over designers who redesign entire apps without evidence of need.
Focused scope demonstrates product thinking — the ability to prioritise what actually needs solving

Key Learnings

What this project
proved

01
Trust is a UX problem, not a technical one
The data to rebuild trust already existed in PickMe's systems. The failure was entirely in how that data was communicated to users — or not communicated at all. Trust issues in digital products are almost always information architecture problems in disguise.
02
Transparent uncertainty beats false precision
Users do not need certainty. They need honesty. An ETA with a range and a reason is infinitely more trustworthy than a precise ETA that changes without explanation. Designing for honest uncertainty is a higher-order UX skill than designing for optimistic precision.
03
Focused scope is a product thinking signal
The deliberate decision to redesign one workflow rather than the whole app demonstrates product maturity. The best product designers know what not to redesign as clearly as they know what to fix. Scope discipline is itself a design skill.
04
BA analysis surfaces the real problem
Without the workflow analysis and business impact mapping, this case study would have been a visual redesign of a tracking screen. The BA layer is what revealed that the root cause was not visual design but information transparency — a fundamentally different problem with a fundamentally different solution.
05
Waiting is an emotional experience, not a neutral one
The 4 minutes between driver acceptance and arrival are not empty time — they are a high-stakes emotional experience. Users are anxious, measuring, and forming judgements about the platform's reliability. Designing for waiting requires designing for emotion, not just efficiency.
06
Mental models are UX infrastructure
Giving users a mental model of how a system works — through journey timelines, status labels, and contextual notifications — reduces cognitive load more effectively than interface simplification alone. When users understand a system, complexity stops feeling complicated.
